## ⏱️ Reality check on books
A 6-stage process rewards your **15 years of real experience** more than freshly-read theory. Don't try to read 5 books. **Skim the top 2, get fluent on agentic concepts, and spend the rest of your time rehearsing STAR stories out loud.**

## 🥇 Highest Leverage — read/skim these first

### 1. Designing Data-Intensive Applications — Martin Kleppmann
**Why:** The single most relevant book. Round 4 (Technical Assessment) likely involves system design. Canonical text for distributed systems, storage, streaming, consistency tradeoffs. You already know much of this from Kafka/Flink/Lakehouse — this sharpens how you *articulate* it.
**Read:** Stream processing + storage/replication chapters.

### 2. The Manager's Path — Camille Fournier
**Why:** Directly targets the management-of-managers scope this role centers on.
**Read:** "Managing Managers" + "Leading Bigger Organizations" chapters. Maps to your 8→30 scaling.

## 🤖 The Agentic / AI Dimension (this role's differentiator — your edge)

No single book needed — you need **current fluency**, not theory. Get sharp on:
- How coding agents work (Claude Code, Codex, Cursor, Copilot, Gemini, Devin — all named in the JD)
- What "code verification" means and why AI-generated code is risky (subtle logic/security/maintainability issues at volume)
- Sonar's **AC/DC** concept (Agent-Centric Development Cycle) and products (SonarQube, Foundation Agent, SonarSweep, Context Augmentation)
- RAG, agentic patterns, guardrails — you've *built* these (Text-to-SQL, agentic AI), so frame from experience

**This is where you win.** Most software-eng candidates can't speak credibly to agentic AI verification. You can.
